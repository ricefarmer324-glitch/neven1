TOPIC 01
Pretérito vs Imperfecto
The two most confused past tenses — here's how to tell them apart
Explanation
Both are past tenses, but they do different jobs.

Pretérito = something that started AND finished. It's done. Complete. Over. You can almost picture a period at the end.

Imperfecto = something that was ongoing, repeated, or described the past. No clear end. Think of it like a background painting — it sets the scene.

Quick test: Ask "Did this have a definite end?" If yes → pretérito. If it was a habit or just setting the scene → imperfecto.
Signal Words
ayer anoche el lunes de repente una vez en 2018 siempre todos los días cuando era niño/a a veces generalmente cada semana

■ Yellow = pretérito    ■ Green = imperfecto

Examples
Ayer comí una pizza entera.
Yesterday I ate a whole pizza. (done — pretérito)
De niño, comía pizza todos los días.
As a kid, I used to eat pizza every day. (habit — imperfecto)
Yo estudiaba cuando ella llamó.
I was studying (background) when she called (event).
El lunes, fui al gimnasio.
On Monday, I went to the gym. (specific day = pretérito)
Hacía frío y llovía cuando salí.
It was cold and raining when I left. (weather description = imperfecto)
Common Mistakes
✕
Using pretérito for habits: Siempre fui al parque → Siempre iba al parque
✕
Forgetting that descriptions (weather, feelings, age, appearance) almost always need imperfecto.
✕
Using imperfecto when a clear time frame is given: A las 3pm yo comía → comí

TOPIC 02
Presente Perfecto vs Pluscuamperfecto
"Have done" vs "had done" — it's all about the order of events
Explanation
Presente Perfecto = "I have done" — something happened in the past but it's still relevant to RIGHT NOW.
Structure: haber (present) + participio → he/has/ha/hemos/habéis/han + comido

Pluscuamperfecto = "I had done" — something happened BEFORE another past event. The further-back past.
Structure: haber (imperfecto) + participio → había/habías/había/habíamos/habíais/habían + comido

Key irregular participles: hecho, dicho, visto, escrito, roto, puesto, vuelto, abierto
Timeline

pluscuamperfecto
→
otra acción pasada
past event
→
he comido
presente perfecto
→
AHORA
now
Examples
He comido sushi tres veces este mes.
I have eaten sushi three times this month. (still "this month" — relevant now)
Nunca he viajado a Europa.
I have never traveled to Europe. (still true today)
Cuando llegué, ella ya había salido.
When I arrived, she had already left. (left BEFORE I arrived)
Él había estudiado antes del examen.
He had studied before the exam. (studying came first)
Ya hemos terminado el proyecto.
We have already finished the project. (relevant to now)
Common Mistakes
✕
Forgetting irregular participles: he vido → he visto, he escribido → he escrito
✕
Using pretérito instead of pluscuamperfecto: Cuando llegué, ella salió (sounds like they happened together) → había salido (she left before)
✕
Mixing up haber conjugations. Presente perfecto uses: he/has/ha/hemos/habéis/han. Pluscuamperfecto uses: había/habías/había/habíamos/habíais/habían.

TOPIC 03
Tiempos en Pasado
All four past tenses side by side — the ultimate comparison
Explanation
Spanish has four main past tenses. Think of them as different camera angles on the past:

🎬 Pretérito — snapshots of completed events
🎨 Imperfecto — the background painting (ongoing / habitual)
🔗 Presente Perfecto — connects the past to right now
⏪ Pluscuamperfecto — the furthest back, before another past event
Comparison Chart
Tense When to use Structure Example
Pretérito Completed, specific, one-time actions stem + é/aste/ó... Fui al médico el martes.
Imperfecto Ongoing, habitual, descriptive past stem + aba/ía... Iba al médico cada semana.
Pres. Perfecto "Have done" — still relevant now he/has/ha + -ado/-ido He ido al médico hoy.
Pluscuamperfecto "Had done" — before another past event había/habías + -ado/-ido Ya había ido cuando llamaste.
Examples — Same Verb, 4 Tenses
Ayer jugué videojuegos.
Pretérito — completed action yesterday
Cuando era niño, jugaba videojuegos.
Imperfecto — ongoing habit in childhood
Esta semana he jugado demasiado.
Presente Perfecto — this week, still ongoing
Ya había jugado dos horas antes de que llegara mamá.
Pluscuamperfecto — played before mom arrived
Common Mistakes
✕
Mixing up all four — when in doubt, ask: Was it habitual? (imperfecto) Did it have a clear end? (pretérito) Is it still relevant today? (presente perfecto) Did it happen before another past event? (pluscuamperfecto)
✕
Using pretérito for everything — don't default to pretérito just because it's past. Always check for habits, background, or sequence.

  • 2.había estudiadobefore exam started
  • 3.he leído"este mes" still active
TOPIC 04
Acentos
When you stress a syllable that breaks the rules, write the accent mark
Explanation
Spanish words are stressed on a specific syllable. There are default rules — if you break them, you write an accent mark (´).

Agudas → stress on the LAST syllable. Write accent only if the word ends in n, s, or vowel.
Example: ca-FÉ ✓, ha-BLÓ ✓, pa-PEL (no accent — ends in L)

Llanas → stress on the SECOND-TO-LAST syllable. Write accent only if the word does NOT end in n, s, or vowel.
Example: CÁ-sil (no — wait, CA-sa = no accent, ends in -a), lá-piz ✓ (ends in z)

Esdrújulas → stress on the THIRD-TO-LAST syllable. ALWAYS write the accent.
Example: MÚ-si-ca ✓, rá-pi-do ✓, fá-bri-ca ✓

Examples
Tú tienes tu libro.
You have your book. (tú = you; tu = your)
La música es esdrújula.
Music — stress 3rd from last = always gets accent
El café está caliente.
Coffee — aguda ending in vowel = needs accent
¿Qué quieres que te diga?
What do you want me to say? — accent in question words
Common Mistakes
✕
Forgetting that ALL esdrújulas ALWAYS have a written accent. No exceptions.
✕
Writing tu when you mean tú (subject pronoun). Ask: "Am I saying 'you' as the person, or 'your' as ownership?"
✕
Forgetting accents on question words in direct AND indirect questions: Dime cómo te llamas (not "como")

TOPIC 05
Frases Impersonales
When there's no specific subject — talking about "people in general"
Explanation
Impersonal phrases are used when the subject is unknown, general, or unimportant — like "one should," "it is important to," or "people say."

se + verb → someone/people (general subject)
hay → there is / there are
es + adjective + que + subjuntivo → It is important that... (triggers subjunctive!)

Key expressions: es importante que, es necesario que, es bueno que, es malo que, es posible que, se dice, se come, hay que + infinitive
Structure Reference
se + verb (3rd person) hay + noun hay que + infinitive es [adj] que + subjuntivo
Examples
Se habla español aquí.
Spanish is spoken here. (no specific speaker)
Hay mucha tarea hoy.
There is a lot of homework today.
Hay que estudiar para el examen.
One must study for the exam. (general obligation)
Es importante que llegues a tiempo.
It's important that you arrive on time. (+ subjunctive!)
Se dice que va a llover mañana.
They say / people say it's going to rain tomorrow.
Common Mistakes
✕
Using indicative after impersonal expressions: Es importante que estudias → Es importante que estudies (subjunctive!)
✕
Confusing hay (there is/are) with es (it is): "Hay importante estudiar" ✗ → Hay que estudiar / Es importante estudiar
✕
Hay que is always followed by an INFINITIVE, never a conjugated verb.
Practice
Multiple Choice · 1
_____ mucho ruido en la cafetería.
Fill in the Blank · 2
Es necesario que tú la tarea. (subjuntivo)
  • 1.Haythere is/are = hay + noun
  • 2.hagases necesario que → triggers subjunctive
TOPIC 06
Por vs Para
Both mean "for" in English — but they are NOT interchangeable
Quick Rule System
POR — reason/cause/exchangePARA — purpose/goal/destination
Reason or cause (because of)Purpose / goal (in order to)
Exchange (in exchange for)Recipient (for someone)
Duration of time (for X hours)Deadline (due by)
Movement through/along a placeDestination (going to)
Means of communication/transportOpinion/perspective (for a…, in the opinion of)
Agent in passive voice (by)Employment (work for)
Examples
Te llamo por teléfono.
I'll call you by phone. (means of comm. → por)
Estudio para sacar buenas notas.
I study in order to get good grades. (purpose → para)
Caminamos por el parque.
We walked through the park. (movement through → por)
El regalo es para mi mamá.
The gift is for my mom. (recipient → para)
Necesito el ensayo para el lunes.
I need the essay by Monday. (deadline → para)
Gracias por tu ayuda.
Thanks for your help. (because of / in exchange for → por)
Tricky Examples Students Confuse
⚠
"I worked for 3 hours" = Trabajé por 3 horas (duration → por) — NOT para
⚠
"I work for Google" = Trabajo para Google (employer → para) — NOT por
⚠
"For a student, he speaks well" = Para ser estudiante, habla bien (comparison/opinion → para)
⚠
"I paid $20 for the shirt" = Pagué $20 por la camisa (exchange → por)

TOPIC 07
Usos de "Se"
One tiny word — four completely different jobs
Explanation
"Se" is one of the hardest words in Spanish because it does very different things depending on context. Learn to recognize each type:

1. Reflexivo — subject acts on itself ("I wash myself")
2. Recíproco — two+ people act on each other ("They love each other")
3. Le → Se — indirect object pronoun swaps to "se" before lo/la/los/las
4. Impersonal / Pasivo — no specific subject ("Spanish is spoken here")
Examples — One Per Type
🪞 Reflexivo: Ella se lava el pelo.
She washes her (own) hair. — She acts on herself
💞 Recíproco: Ellos se quieren mucho.
They love each other a lot. — Action is mutual
📦 Le→Se: Se lo di a Juan. (Le lo → Se lo)
I gave it to Juan. — "le" becomes "se" before "lo"
🌍 Impersonal: Se habla español aquí.
Spanish is spoken here. — No specific subject
Common Mistakes
✕
Writing le lo instead of se lo: "Le lo dije" → Se lo dije (two L-pronouns can't go together)
✕
Confusing reflexive with impersonal: "Yo me habla" ✗ — reflexive means YOU act on YOURSELF. Impersonal "se" has no subject and uses 3rd person verb.
✕
Forgetting that reciprocal "se" requires a PLURAL subject: "Se quiere" (just one person) ✗ vs Se quieren (they love each other) ✓
Practice
Multiple Choice · 1
¿Qué tipo de "se" es? — "Se venden zapatos aquí."
Fill in the Blank · 2

  • 1.Impersonal / Pasivono specific subject, passive meaning
  • 2.se lole + lo → se lo (two L-pronouns)
TOPIC 08
Subjuntivo
The mood of doubt, emotion, and influence — learn the triggers
Explanation
The subjunctive isn't a tense — it's a mood. You use it when the main clause expresses wishes, emotions, doubt, or influence over another person.

Formation: Start with the yo-form of present tense → drop the -o → add opposite endings.
-AR verbs → add -e endings (hable, hables, hable...)
-ER/-IR verbs → add -a endings (coma, comas, coma...)

Key rule: Subjunctive usually appears in the SECOND clause, after "que," when the subjects are DIFFERENT.
WEIRDO Triggers
W
Wishes
querer que, desear que, esperar que
E
Emotions
alegrarse de que, tener miedo de que, sorprender que
I
Impersonal
es importante que, es necesario que, es bueno que
R
Recommendations
recomendar que, sugerir que, aconsejar que
D
Doubt/Denial
dudar que, no creer que, negar que
O
Ojala
ojalá (que) — always triggers subjunctive
Examples
Quiero que tú estudies más.
I want you to study more. (Wish — different subjects)
Me alegra que vengas a la fiesta.
I'm glad you're coming to the party. (Emotion)
Es importante que todos lleguen a tiempo.
It's important that everyone arrive on time. (Impersonal)
Dudo que él sepa la respuesta.
I doubt he knows the answer. (Doubt)
¡Ojalá haga buen tiempo mañana!
Hopefully the weather will be nice tomorrow!
Common Mistakes
✕
Using subjunctive when subjects are THE SAME: Quiero que vaya (if I want MYSELF to go) → Quiero ir (infinitive — same subject)
✕
Using indicative after "creer" in affirmative (no trigger) vs negative (trigger): "Creo que es verdad" (indicative ✓) vs "No creo que sea verdad" (subjunctive ✓)
✕
Irregular yo-form = irregular subjunctive. tener → tengo → tenga. salir → salgo → salga.
Practice
Multiple Choice · 1
Mi mamá quiere que yo _____ (limpiar) mi cuarto.
Multiple Choice · 2
No creo que él _____ (tener) razón.
Fill in the Blank · 3
Espero que tú a mi cumpleaños. (subjuntivo)
  • 1.limpiequerer que → wish trigger, -ar verb → -e ending
  • 2.tengano creer que → doubt trigger; tener → tengo → tenga
  • 3.vengasesperar que → wish; venir is irregular: vengas
TOPIC 09
Pretérito Perfecto de Subjuntivo
When something already happened — but the main clause still cares about it
Explanation
This tense = the subjunctive version of "presente perfecto." Use it when:
1. The main clause has a WEIRDO trigger (same as regular subjunctive)
2. The action in the subordinate clause has already happened (or will have happened by the time the main clause is true)

Structure: haya / hayas / haya / hayamos / hayáis / hayan + participio

Think: "I'm glad that you have come" vs "I want you to come" (regular subjunctive)
Timeline
haya llegado
already happened
→
main clause
espero / me alegra
→
AHORA
now / future
Examples
Espero que él haya llegado bien.
I hope he has arrived safely. (he may have already arrived)
Me alegra que hayas aprobado el examen.
I'm glad you have passed the exam. (already happened)
Es bueno que lo hayas dicho.
It's good that you said it. (already said)
No creo que ellos hayan terminado todavía.
I don't think they have finished yet.
Common Mistakes
✕
Using regular subjunctive when the action is already completed: "Me alegra que vengas" (coming in the future) vs "Me alegra que hayas venido" (already came)
✕
Confusing haber conjugations: it's haya (subjunctive of "ha") — NOT "ha" or "había"
✕
Forgetting irregular participles still apply: haya hecho, haya dicho, haya visto
